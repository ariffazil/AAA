## PETRONAS: A Crisis of Stewardship

PETRONAS — Petroliam Nasional Berhad — was established in 1974 under the Petroleum Development Act. For nearly five decades it has been Malaysia's primary source of federal revenue, contributing on average 25-30% of total government income. Today, the company faces an unprecedented convergence of pressures that test its founding compact: that Malaysia's hydrocarbon wealth belongs to Malaysians and is to be managed in their long-term interest.

### The Dividend Squeeze

Federal dividend receipts from PETRONAS peaked around RM50 billion in 2022, when Brent crude prices averaged over $100 per barrel and the post-pandemic recovery boosted downstream margins. By 2024, with crude prices normalizing in the $70-80 range and rightsizing programmes biting into production, dividend payments had compressed to roughly RM32 billion. The 2025-2026 trajectory suggests a further decline toward RM20-25 billion — a near-halving in three years.

This is not merely a commodity cycle. The deeper issue is that PETRONAS's production base has shrunk while its cost base has expanded. Malaysia's domestic crude production, around 750,000 barrels per day in 2018, has fallen to roughly 478,000 bpd in early 2026 — a 36% decline driven by natural field depletion and the absence of major new discoveries since the 2010s.

### The Divestment Pattern

The most visible strategic shift is the move toward joint ventures and divestitures:

- In November 2025, at ADIPEC in Abu Dhabi, PETRONAS announced a $15 billion JV with Italy's Eni covering 19 upstream assets — 14 in Indonesia (Kutei Basin core) and 5 in Malaysia (including Kasawari). Eni takes a 50% stake in the UK-incorporated special purpose vehicle, with operations beginning July 2026.

- In June 2026, PETRONAS sold four mature Peninsular and Sarawak PSCs to UK-listed EnQuest for approximately $833 million. The deal value is modest in absolute terms but symbolically significant: it represents the first time PETRONAS has divested operational control of producing assets to a European operator at this scale.

These transactions share three features: (1) the foreign counterparty dictates the legal architecture (UK domicile, English law, London arbitration), (2) Malaysia retains only an economic interest, not operational control, and (3) the Sarawak state oil company PETROS holds no equity in any of the upstream vehicles, despite Sarawak contributing the majority of Malaysia's gas supply.

### The Sarawak Question

Sarawak supplies roughly 90% of Malaysian LNG through the Bintulu complex. The state government, asserting rights under the Malaysia Agreement 1963 (MA63), established PETROS in 2017 and has been issuing gas invoices directly to operators, bypassing PETRONAS. The Federal Court in March 2026 allowed PETRONAS's constitutional challenge to proceed — a case that will take years to resolve, but which raises the fundamental question of who owns Malaysia's subsoil hydrocarbons.

The economic stakes are real. Industry analysts estimate the interpleader litigation has been costing RM70-80 million per month in disputed royalty payments. Sarawak argues for a 20% royalty rate under MA63; PETRONAS continues to pay 5% under the Petroleum Development Act 1974. The difference, compounded over decades, runs into the tens of billions of ringgit.

### The Energy Transition Pressure

Globally, oil and gas companies face a structural transition risk. The EU's Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) currently covers cement, steel, aluminium, fertilizers and electricity — not yet LNG. But the regulatory direction is clear: hydrocarbon exports face increasing carbon-intensity scrutiny, particularly from European and Japanese buyers who account for a significant portion of Malaysian LNG demand.

Domestically, the government has signalled via the National Energy Transition Roadmap (NETR) that Malaysia must reduce emissions intensity by 45% by 2030. PETRONAS's clean energy subsidiary, Gentari, has been positioned as the vehicle for renewable investments. However, the Gentari segment reportedly burned RM1-1.5 billion annually since its 2022 launch without generating meaningful profit — a drain on group capital during a period when upstream margins are already compressed.

### The Governance Question

Unlike publicly listed companies, PETRONAS has no independent shareholders who can challenge management decisions. The Prime Minister is also Finance Minister; the chairman is selected by government; the auditor is appointed by management. There is no requirement for quarterly analyst calls, no public segment reporting beyond what is required by accounting standards, and no obligation to seek shareholder approval for transactions above a certain size.

This concentrated governance structure is not unique to Malaysia. Other national oil companies (Saudi Aramco, Petronas Indonesia, Petrobras) operate under similar constraints. But the absence of external accountability becomes particularly acute during periods of strategic transition, when the temptation to monetize the crown jewels for short-term fiscal relief is hardest to resist.

### What Should an Ordinary Malaysian Do?

Five concrete actions:

1. Email your parliamentary representative. Ask whether Cabinet approved the EnQuest transaction; whether the Sarawak royalty dispute will be settled by negotiation or litigation; whether the 20% MA63 claim has been formally costed; whether PETRONAS's clean energy investments are yielding measurable emissions reductions; and what the projected 2027-2030 dividend trajectory looks like under different oil price scenarios.

2. File a public interest complaint with the Malaysian Anti-Corruption Commission (MACC/SPRM) if you have direct evidence of impropriety in the licensing or sale of upstream assets. Whistleblower protections under the Whistleblower Protection Act 2010 apply.

3. In Sarawak, raise the issue through your community leader (ketua kaum/JKKK) and ask your State Assembly representative (ADUN) whether the state supports the 20% MA63 claim and whether PETROS should hold equity in any future upstream joint ventures.

4. Write to editors at Borneo Post, Malaysiakini, The Edge, or Free Malaysia Today with documented facts and sources. The mainstream media has not provided adequate coverage of these transactions; informed public attention is the most effective constraint on unaccountable decision-making.

5. Track the public filings. PETRONAS Annual Reports are deposited with the Companies Commission of Malaysia (SSM). The Auditor General's Report is tabled in Parliament. Court documents from the MA63 case are public. Read them directly rather than relying on summaries.

### The Bottom Line

PETRONAS is not collapsing. It has substantial reserves, capable management, and a sovereign backstop. But the institution is undergoing a fundamental renegotiation — with foreign partners who dictate the legal terms, with state governments who assert ownership claims, with global markets that increasingly price carbon risk, and with Malaysian citizens who are watching their national patrimony become a portfolio rebalancing exercise.

The question is not whether PETRONAS will survive. It will. The question is whether the value created will accrue to Malaysians or to the counterparties who structure these transactions. That depends on governance, transparency, and public engagement. The 5-year capex cycle of the Eni deal commits Malaysia to a relationship that will outlast the current government, the current dividend cycle, and possibly the current commodity supercycle. Once structural concessions are made, they are very difficult to unwind.
